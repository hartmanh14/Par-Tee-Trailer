# Market Desire Validation Guide - Triple Play Mobile Golf

Created: 2026-06-15  
Purpose: determine whether Troy/Dayton-area customers will actually book, sponsor, or host a mobile golf simulator trailer before Hayden commits nonrefundable money to trailer purchase and retrofit.

## 1. What You Are Validating

The current concept is not a three-in-one entertainment trailer at launch. The launch offer is:

- A mobile golf simulator trailer delivered to events.
- Default configuration: group golf lounge with built-in participant seating.
- Fundraiser configuration: seating swapped for a sign-in desk / management hub.
- Core fundraiser games: closest-to-the-pin and longest-drive competitions.
- Future movie/gaming configurations are Phase 2 only.

The validation question is not "Do people think this is cool?" The question is:

> Will enough local buyers pay enough, often enough, with manageable setup constraints, to justify buying and retrofitting the trailer?

## 2. Decision Standard

Do not buy or place a nonrefundable deposit on a trailer until there is evidence in all five categories:

| Category | Minimum Evidence Before Trailer Deposit |
|---|---|
| Customer demand | 30+ serious conversations across target segments |
| Willingness to pay | 3+ written soft commitments, refundable deposits, or specific event-date quote requests |
| Fundraiser demand | 3+ nonprofit/school/booster conversations where the contact agrees the format could fit an actual event |
| Build feasibility | Trailer/tow/swing/payload/power constraints pass a written spec gate |
| Risk feasibility | 2 insurance quote ranges and no obvious exclusion that breaks the operating model |

Strong go signal:
- 5+ warm bookings or refundable deposits.
- At least 2 nonprofit/fundraiser hosts willing to test the format.
- At least 1 corporate/HR/event planner interested in a weekday activation.
- Trailer quotes, simulator quote, and insurance are still inside the Lean budget path.

No-go or pause signal:
- People like the idea but will not name a real event, budget, date, or decision-maker.
- Most prospects compare it only to cheap hourly indoor simulator bays.
- Venue/setup/towing/insurance constraints create high friction.
- Fundraiser hosts like the attraction but cannot see how it raises more than it costs.

## 3. Evidence Ladder

Treat evidence by strength. Polite enthusiasm is cheap.

| Evidence Type | Strength | How To Interpret |
|---|---:|---|
| "That sounds fun" | Weak | Nice, but not useful by itself |
| Shares a real event they might use it for | Moderate | Worth follow-up |
| Names a budget range without prompting | Moderate | Indicates actual buying frame |
| Asks about availability, requirements, or deposit | Strong | Buying process has started |
| Introduces you to decision-maker | Strong | Internal champion exists |
| Signs letter of intent or gives refundable deposit | Very strong | Real validation |
| Sponsor agrees to fund a challenge | Very strong | Fundraiser economics validated |

## 4. Core Hypotheses To Test

### H1 - Private Event Demand

People in the Troy/Dayton service radius will pay about $499-$699 for a 2-hour mobile golf simulator experience for parties, tailgates, driveway nights, and adult gatherings.

What would validate it:
- 10+ private-event interviews.
- 4+ prospects name a likely event within 12 months.
- 2+ ask for launch notification, quote, or date check.

### H2 - Fundraiser Demand

Schools, athletic boosters, nonprofits, churches, and civic groups will use a mobile golf challenge to raise money through donation entries, sponsor prizes, and leaderboard visibility.

What would validate it:
- 10+ fundraiser-host interviews.
- 3+ say they would pilot closest-to-pin or longest-drive.
- 2+ can identify a sponsor category or donor audience.
- At least 1 agrees to a tentative demo date or planning call.

### H3 - Corporate Demand

HR, office managers, and event planners will pay $1,500+ for a mobile golf activation for employee appreciation, client entertainment, company family days, or workplace giving.

What would validate it:
- 5+ corporate conversations.
- 2+ ask for an event quote or internal proposal language.
- 1+ agrees to consider a weekday demo or pilot.

### H4 - Operational Feasibility

The trailer can safely support simulator play, group seating, and fundraiser desk configuration without setup friction killing demand.

What would validate it:
- Tow vehicle and trailer ratings pass.
- Swing clearance and payload pass.
- Generator/shore power plan passes.
- Insurance broker does not flag a fatal exclusion.

### H5 - Differentiation

Customers understand why a mobile simulator trailer is worth more than an hourly indoor simulator bay.

What would validate it:
- Prospects repeat back benefits like "comes to us," "private," "group-friendly," "fundraiser attraction," "weather resistant," or "sponsor friendly."
- Price objections are about budget/timing, not confusion over what it is.

Run the first validation sprint with at least 35 conversations.

| Segment | Minimum Interviews | Primary Question | Best Signal |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Adult golfers / private party hosts | 8 | Would they book a mobile simulator for a real gathering? | Date, budget, guest count |

## 6. The Interview Rules

Do:
- Ask about their last event before pitching your idea.
- Ask what they paid for alternatives.
- Ask who makes the decision.
- Ask what would block them from booking.
- Ask for a specific next step if interest is real.

Do not:
- Lead with "Wouldn't this be awesome?"
- Ask friends/family only.
- Accept compliments as validation.
- Hide the likely price.
- Ask "Would you pay?" without asking about a real event.

## 7. Interview Script - Private Party / Golf Host

Opening:

"I'm testing a golf-first mobile simulator trailer concept for Troy/Dayton events. Before I spend money building it, I'm trying to learn whether people would actually use it. Can I ask you a few questions about events you host or attend?"

Questions:

1. What was the last party, tailgate, work event, or golf-related gathering you hosted or helped plan?
2. What did you do for entertainment?
3. What did it cost, roughly?
4. What was frustrating or hard about planning it?
5. If a private golf simulator trailer came to your driveway or venue, who would use it?
6. Would this be more useful for casual play, a competition, practice, or background entertainment?
7. For a 2-hour hosted event starting around $499, would this be in range, too expensive, or surprisingly affordable?
8. What would have to be true for you to book it?
9. Is there a real event in the next 6-12 months where this could fit?
10. If I build it, would you want a launch quote or demo invite?

Strong close:

"If I offered a refundable launch deposit to hold an early booking date, would you consider it, or is that premature?"

## 8. Interview Script - Nonprofit / Booster / Church

Opening:

"I'm validating a mobile golf simulator fundraiser format: closest-to-the-pin or longest-drive contests where guests donate for attempts, sponsors fund prizes or the leaderboard, and the trailer acts as the event attraction. I'm not selling yet; I'm trying to learn whether this would actually help groups raise money."

Questions:

1. What fundraisers have worked best for your group?
2. What fundraisers have been too much work for the money raised?
3. How many attendees or families do you usually reach?
4. Do local businesses sponsor your events?
5. Would a golf challenge fit your audience, or would it feel too niche?
6. Which format is easier to imagine: closest-to-the-pin, longest-drive, or both?
7. Would guests pay $10-$25 per entry attempt if prizes/sponsors were involved?
8. Would you prefer flat rental, revenue share, sponsor-paid, or no-risk pilot?
9. What venue or event could physically host a trailer?
10. Who else would need to approve this?

Strong close:

"Would it be worth a 20-minute planning call to sketch a pilot fundraiser and estimate gross raised?"

## 9. Interview Script - Corporate / HR / Event Planner

Opening:

"I'm validating a mobile golf simulator trailer for employee appreciation, client events, and workplace giving campaigns in the Miami Valley. It would be a hosted golf bay brought to your site, with competition formats if needed."

Questions:

1. What kinds of employee or client events do you plan?
2. What activities have worked well?
3. What activities have felt stale or hard to justify?
4. What budget range is typical for a half-day activation?
5. Would golf be too narrow, or useful because people can rotate in quickly?
6. Would a closest-to-pin leaderboard work for employee engagement or charity giving?
7. What insurance, COI, parking, or vendor requirements would apply?
8. Who approves this type of spend?
9. Would $1,500-$3,500 be plausible for the right event?
10. What would you need in a proposal to consider it?

Strong close:

"Can I send a one-page draft proposal and ask you to mark up what would or would not get approved?"

## 10. Smoke Tests Before Spending

Use these to separate curiosity from demand.

### Smoke Test A - Launch Interest Form

Create a simple form with:
- Name
- Email/phone
- Event type
- City
- Estimated date
- Guest count
- Interested package: Hosted Golf Bay, Fundraiser Challenge, Corporate Activation
- "Would you consider a refundable launch deposit?"

Pass threshold:
- 20+ qualified leads in 30 days.
- 5+ ask for quote/date.
- 2+ willing to discuss deposit.

### Smoke Test B - Fundraiser Pilot Offer

Offer 3 pilot fundraiser slots:
- Reduced flat fee or sponsor-paid format.
- Clear statement that it is a pilot pending trailer/build timeline.
- Ask for event date, venue, attendance, sponsor prospects, and decision-maker.

Pass threshold:
- 3+ groups take a planning call.
- 1+ group agrees to be a first pilot if build proceeds.

### Smoke Test C - Sponsor Package Test

Pitch local businesses:
- $250 prize sponsor
- $500 leaderboard sponsor
- $1,000 presenting sponsor

Pass threshold:
- 3+ businesses say the package is understandable.
- 1+ says they would likely sponsor for the right organization/event.

### Smoke Test D - Paid Reservation Test

Only after legal language is reviewed:
- Offer refundable $100 launch deposits for early access.
- Make refund terms clear.
- Do not imply the trailer is built or insured if it is not.

Pass threshold:
- 3+ refundable deposits from people outside close family.

Questions to answer:

- What do mobile party vendors charge for 2 hours?
- Are corporate/event activations priced differently?
- Do any competitors support fundraisers directly?
- Are customers already trained to pay for mobile entertainment?

## 13. Fundraiser Economics Test

The fundraiser must be easy to understand:

Gross raised = participant entries + sponsor dollars

Example:
- 80 participants x $20 entries = $1,600
- Sponsor/prize money = $500
- Estimated gross = $2,100

But the real question is net value:

Net value = gross raised - trailer/event cost - payment fees - prize cost - volunteer time

Ask each fundraiser contact:
- What is a successful net amount for this type of event?
- Would the trailer fee be paid by sponsor, organization, or revenue split?
- Who collects the money?
- Would repeat attempts be allowed?
- What prizes would motivate people?
- Would youth/adult divisions help?

## 14. Validation Scorecard

Score each category 0-5. Do not buy the trailer unless the total is at least 22/30 and no P0 blocker remains.

| Category | 0 | 3 | 5 | Score |
|---|---|---|---|---:|
| Private event demand | No real events named | Some interest, few specifics | Multiple quote/date requests |  |
| Fundraiser demand | Polite but vague | Some event fit | Pilot host or sponsor identified |  |
| Corporate demand | No budget fit | Proposal interest | Real decision-maker requests quote |  |
| Price acceptance | Mostly too expensive | Mixed but plausible | Buyers accept draft prices |  |
| Operational feasibility | Major unknowns | Some constraints known | Tow/trailer/power/insurance path viable |  |
| Competitive differentiation | Seen as novelty only | Some clear benefits | Prospects repeat strong reasons to buy |  |

Decision:
- 26-30: proceed if build quotes fit.
- 22-25: proceed cautiously; reduce scope and keep deposits refundable.
- 18-21: pause and keep validating.
- Under 18: do not buy trailer.

## 16. Objection Log

Track objections because they tell you what to fix.

| Objection | What It Might Mean | Response / Test |
|---|---|---|
| "Golf is too niche" | Need broader social/fundraiser framing | Test closest-to-pin for non-golfers |
| "Too expensive" | Wrong segment, weak value, or true price issue | Ask what they paid for last event |
| "Where would it park?" | Setup friction is real | Create venue pre-check sheet |
| "Our donors are older/younger" | Format mismatch | Test team divisions or sponsor-paid prize |
| "We already have a golf outing" | Could be complementary or redundant | Pitch as after-party, rain plan, or sponsor booth |
| "We need insurance/COI" | Normal B2B requirement | Get broker quotes before paid bookings |

## 17. Recommended 30-Day Validation Sprint

### Week 1 - Prepare

- Finalize one-page pitch for three segments: private, fundraiser, corporate.
- Build a simple lead form.
- Make a 50-prospect list.
- Confirm trailer quote questions.
- Confirm insurance broker questions.

### Week 2 - Private And Golf Demand

- Complete 8-10 adult golfer/private event interviews.
- Quote-shop fixed simulator competitors.
- Post in 2-3 local groups only if allowed.
- Ask for launch waitlist or refundable deposit interest.

### Week 3 - Fundraiser Demand

- Contact 10 nonprofits, schools, churches, boosters, and civic groups.
- Run 5+ real conversations.
- Ask each contact to identify a likely sponsor and event date.
- Build one sample fundraiser math sheet for each interested group.

### Week 4 - Corporate And Go/No-Go

- Contact 10 HR/office/event planner prospects.
- Complete 5+ conversations.
- Get insurance quote ranges and trailer quotes.
- Score the validation scorecard.
- Write one-page go/no-go memo.

## 18. Best Early Prospect Types

Start with groups that already understand events and sponsors:

- Athletic boosters.
- Golf teams.
- School foundations.
- Youth sports teams.
- Churches with youth trips.
- Civic clubs.
- Local charities with donor nights.
- Corporate giving committees.
- Breweries or venues with parking and event traffic.
- Golf outing organizers.

Avoid starting with groups that have no event infrastructure, no sponsor base, and no clear decision-maker.

## 20. What To Decide After Validation

At the end of the sprint, write a one-page memo:

1. Number of interviews completed.
2. Number of strong leads.
3. Best segment.
4. Worst segment.
5. Best price signal.
6. Biggest objection.
7. Trailer quote range.
8. Insurance quote range.
9. Whether fundraiser economics work.
10. Decision: proceed, pause, pivot, or stop.
